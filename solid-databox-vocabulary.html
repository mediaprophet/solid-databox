This vocabulary defines the content categories and metadata used by Solid-Databox [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]] to type the records an institution deposits about a person: from a letter or a case note, through economic records and structured system data, to telephony and multimedia — IVR interactions, call recordings, interviews, and body-worn video. It reuses PROV-O [[PROV-O]], Dublin Core [[DCTERMS]], schema.org [[SCHEMA-ORG]], and the Verifiable Credentials data model [[VC-DATA-MODEL-2.0]], and coins terms (prefix sop:) only where those leave a gap — most importantly for the relationship between an original record and its derivatives, such as transcripts, whose accuracy cannot be assumed.

Unofficial draft, no standing at any standards body, published to structure work toward the Solid 2026 Hackathon. Companion to the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]] and [[SOLID-DATABOX-PRIMER]]. The namespace https://w3id.org/solid-databox/ns# (prefix sop:) is provisional.

This document defines classes and properties, and a small number of usage rules for them. Where those rules use RFC 2119 keywords they bind any implementation that produces or consumes Solid-Databox records; behavioural enforcement is specified in the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]].

Design Principles

  1. Reuse over reinvention. Anchor on established vocabularies; coin sop: terms only for genuine gaps.
  2. The artifact is not its claims. An audio recording is one entity; a transcript asserting what was said is a separate, derived entity with its own author, accuracy, and verification status. A derivative never silently replaces its source.
  3. Three orthogonal axes. A record is typed by modality (its form), by function (its role), and, if derived, by derivation. Instances are multiply typed — a sop:CallRecording may also be a sop:Determination.
  4. Metadata scales with stakes and modality. Every record carries descriptive and provenance metadata; multimedia additionally carries technical and segment-level metadata.
  5. Extensible by institutions. The category set is a SKOS scheme [[SKOS]] that institutions extend for their own record types without breaking interoperability.

Core Class

sop:Record is the unit of egress: any content deposited about a person. It is a subclass of prov:Entity and schema:CreativeWork, so every record carries provenance and descriptive metadata. All classes below are subclasses of sop:Record.

Axis 1 — Modality

How the content is embodied. Aligned with schema.org media types.

sop:TextualRecord (⊑ schema:TextDigitalDocument)
Human-readable text. Subtypes: sop:Letter (formal correspondence), sop:Note / sop:CaseNote (a note made by a person, e.g. a caseworker file note), sop:Form (a completed form), sop:Message (⊑ schema:Message), sop:EmailMessage (⊑ schema:EmailMessage).
sop:AudioRecord (⊑ schema:AudioObject)
Recorded audio. Subtypes: sop:CallRecording (a recorded telephone call), sop:IVRInteraction (an interactive-voice-response session, including DTMF/menu selections and any spoken input), sop:Voicemail, sop:VoiceNote.
sop:VideoRecord (⊑ schema:VideoObject)
Recorded video. Subtypes: sop:Interview, sop:TelehealthConsultation, sop:BodyWornVideo, sop:SurveillanceRecording.
sop:ImageRecord (⊑ schema:ImageObject)
Still images. Subtypes: sop:ScannedDocument, sop:Photograph, sop:IdentityDocumentImage.
sop:StructuredDataRecord (⊑ dcat:Dataset)
Machine-structured data extracted from or held in a system of record. Subtypes: sop:DatabaseRecord (one or more rows), sop:LogEntry, sop:SensorReading.
sop:CompositeRecord
A bundle of related records — e.g. a case file — linked by prov:hadMember / dcterms:hasPart.

Axis 2 — Function

The role a record plays, independent of its modality. Applied alongside a modality type.

sop:Correspondence
A communication between parties (a letter, an email, a call, a message).
sop:Determination
A decision, assessment, or order with consequences for the person — a benefit determination, an eligibility assessment, a court order. High-stakes by default.
sop:CaseNote
An observation or note recorded by an institutional agent about the person or an interaction.
sop:EconomicRecord
A financial record. Subtypes: sop:TransactionRecord, sop:Receipt, sop:Invoice, sop:PaymentRecord, sop:DebtRecord, sop:EntitlementRecord (a voucher, benefit, or concession).
sop:EvidenceItem
A record held or presented as evidence in a proceeding.
sop:IdentityAssertion
A record establishing or asserting an attribute of the person's identity.
sop:HealthRecord, sop:LegalRecord, sop:EducationRecord
Domain records whose access grade is set by their domain profile (see [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]], Access Grades).
sop:BiometricRecord
A special-category record derived from a person's biometrics; the highest access grade and strictest handling apply.

Axis 3 — Derivation, and the Fidelity of Transcripts

A sop:Derivative is a sop:Record produced from another record. It relates to its source by prov:wasDerivedFrom and additionally by sop:assertsContentOf, which makes explicit that the derivative is a claim about the source, not a substitute for it. Subtypes: sop:Transcript, sop:Translation, sop:Summary, sop:Redaction, sop:ExtractedData (e.g. entities or figures pulled from a document), and sop:Proxy (a thumbnail or low-fidelity copy).

A transcript of an IVR interaction or a call is the paradigm case: it may be produced by automatic speech recognition and be materially wrong. Every sop:Derivative therefore MUST declare how it was produced and how far it has been verified:

sop:derivationMethod → sop:DerivationMethod
One of sop:HumanAuthored, sop:MachineGenerated (ASR, OCR, or machine translation), sop:HybridReviewed (machine-generated then human-corrected).
sop:derivationAgent → prov:Agent
The producer — a named person, or software identified by name, version, and (for a model) the model identifier. Also expressible as prov:wasAttributedTo.
sop:verificationStatus → sop:VerificationStatus
One of sop:Unverified, sop:MachineAsserted (produced by a machine, unchecked), sop:HumanReviewed, sop:Certified (attested by an authorised party, e.g. a court reporter), sop:Disputed, or sop:Superseded.
sop:confidence (xsd:decimal, 0–1)
An overall confidence score where the producer supplies one; segment-level confidence is attached via annotation (below).
sop:isVerbatim (xsd:boolean)
Whether the derivative is verbatim or cleaned/edited.
sop:fidelityNote (text)
A human-readable caveat about known limits (audio quality, accent handling, redaction).

Usage rule (enforced by the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]]): a party MUST NOT rely on a derivative at an evidentiary weight its sop:verificationStatus does not support. A sop:MachineAsserted transcript may inform, but MUST NOT by itself ground, a sop:Determination. Where a derivative and its source disagree, the source governs; the original recording is retained and addressable so the claim can always be checked against it.

Metadata Bundles

Each record carries metadata drawn from the following, using reused terms where they exist.

Descriptive
dcterms:title, dcterms:description, dcterms:subject, dcterms:created, dcterms:creator, dcterms:language.
Technical (fuller for multimedia)
schema:encodingFormat (an IANA media type), schema:contentSize, schema:duration (ISO 8601), schema:bitrate, sample rate and codec, image resolution. Integrity via sop:contentHash (an algorithm-prefixed digest, e.g. sha256:…) and, for preservation, premis:fixity.
Provenance
prov:wasGeneratedBy (the capturing or producing activity), prov:wasAttributedTo, prov:wasDerivedFrom, prov:generatedAtTime, prov:wasAssociatedWith.
Capture context
sop:originatingChannel → sop:OriginatingChannel, one of sop:TelephonyIVR, sop:VoiceCall, sop:SMS, sop:Email, sop:WebForm, sop:InPerson, sop:PostalMail, sop:CaseworkerEntry, sop:AutomatedSystem; plus sop:sourceSystem (the system of record) and sop:capturedBy (→ prov:Agent).
Rights & access
sop:accessGrade and the minimum assurance the record requires (see [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]], Access Grades); odrl:hasPolicy for finer permissions.

Multimedia: Segments, Alignment & Correction

Because a wrong record must be challengeable at the point where it is wrong, multimedia records are addressable at the segment level:

  • A span of audio or video is referenced with a Media Fragments URI [[MEDIA-FRAGMENTS]] (for example …/call.wav#t=30,45).
  • A transcript segment carries its source time-alignment, so any line can be played back against the original.
  • A correction or dispute is a Web Annotation [[WEB-ANNOTATION]] whose oa:hasTarget is the specific segment (of the recording or of the transcript) and whose oa:hasBody carries the corrected content — feeding directly into the protocol's Correction return-channel.

Participants, Roles & Interactions

An institution's agents — call-centre operators especially — often identify themselves to a caller by only a first name, or not at all, and a single call may pass through an IVR, one or more operators, a supervisor, and an interpreter before it ends. If a record or a decision flows from that call, the person affected must be able to reconstruct who was involved and when. This vocabulary therefore models the participants of an interaction, their roles, and the handovers between them.

sop:Interaction (⊑ prov:Activity, schema:InteractAction)
A communicative episode between a person and an institution: a call, an IVR session, a chat, or a counter visit. A record such as a sop:CallRecording documents an interaction via sop:documentsInteraction (aligned with prov:wasGeneratedBy).
sop:InteractionSegment (⊑ prov:Activity) — a “leg”
A sub-interval of an interaction with a stable set of participants, bounded by prov:startedAtTime / prov:endedAtTime (or a time:Interval) and linked to its interaction by dcterms:isPartOf. A sop:Hold is a segment with no active sop:InstitutionalAgent.
sop:Participation (⊑ prov:Association)
One party's involvement in an interaction or segment, reifying the agent, the role, and the interval. Attached to the activity by prov:qualifiedAssociation; carries prov:agent, prov:hadRole (a sop:ParticipantRole), and timing.
sop:ParticipantRole
One of sop:Caller (the person, i.e. the Holder), sop:InstitutionalAgent (a human operator representing the institution), sop:AutomatedAgent (an IVR, bot, or AI system), sop:Supervisor, sop:Interpreter, sop:ThirdParty. The participant's type is a prov:Person, prov:SoftwareAgent, or prov:Organization; the role is its part in this interaction.

Every institutional participant is identified, even where their name was not given to the caller:

sop:actorIdentifier
A stable identifier for an institutional participant (an operator or staff ID, or a software agent's identifier) that the institution can resolve to the natural person or software agent it denotes. Required for accountability; pseudonymity is permitted, anonymity is not.
sop:identifierDisclosed (xsd:boolean)
Whether the identifier or the actor's name was disclosed to the person during the interaction.
sop:resolvableBy
The party — the institution, and on lawful request an oversight body or court — able to resolve a pseudonymous sop:actorIdentifier to a natural person.
prov:actedOnBehalfOf, org:member [[ORG]]
The delegation chain: the operator acted on behalf of a department, which is part of the agency; a bot or AI acted on behalf of the institution that deployed it.
sop:modelIdentifier
For a sop:AutomatedAgent, the software name, version, and (for a model) its identifier — so an automated actor is never mistakable for a human.

Handovers are explicit:

sop:TransferEvent (⊑ prov:Activity)
A handover from one participation or segment to the next: sop:transferredFrom and sop:transferredTo, with prov:atTime, an optional sop:transferReason, and an optional target queue or unit (sop:targetUnit → org:Organization). The chain of transfer events makes the full path of a call reconstructable.
<#call-2026-07-02> a sop:IVRInteraction ; sop:documentsInteraction <#int-1> .

<#int-1> a sop:Interaction ;
    prov:qualifiedAssociation <#p-caller> , <#p-ivr> , <#p-op> , <#p-sup> .

<#p-op> a sop:Participation ;
    prov:agent          <https://agency.example/staff/4471> ;   # resolvable by the agency
    prov:hadRole        sop:InstitutionalAgent ;
    sop:actorIdentifier "OP-4471" ; sop:identifierDisclosed false ;
    prov:actedOnBehalfOf <https://agency.example/dept/payments> ;
    prov:startedAtTime  "2026-07-02T09:15:10+10:00"^^xsd:dateTime ;
    prov:endedAtTime    "2026-07-02T09:19:02+10:00"^^xsd:dateTime .

<#t-1> a sop:TransferEvent ; sop:transferredFrom <#p-op> ; sop:transferredTo <#p-sup> ;
    sop:transferReason "escalation" ; prov:atTime "2026-07-02T09:19:02+10:00"^^xsd:dateTime .

Organisations, Sectors & Service Delivery

Records are deposited by, and interactions conducted with, organisations — and increasingly the organisation a person deals with is not the one ultimately responsible. A government service may be delivered by a private contractor or a non-profit under a contract or a statutory delegation. The party model therefore types organisations, their sector, and the delivery chains that connect a delivering party to the principal it acts for, reusing the Organization Ontology [[ORG]] and, for registered bodies, the Registered Organization Vocabulary [[REGORG]].

The organisation and its identity

sop:Organisation is an org:FormalOrganization; where legally registered it is also a rov:RegisteredOrganization carrying a rov:legalName and a rov:registration (for example an Australian ABN or ACN). As with an institutional actor, an organisation MUST be identifiable — a sop:orgIdentifier such as a Legal Entity Identifier (ISO 17442) or a jurisdictional register entry — so that a party cannot be obscured across a delivery chain.

Sector, legal form, and control

An organisation is typed on three independent facets, because what a body does, how it is incorporated, and who controls or funds it are different questions: a charity may deliver a health service under government funding.

  • Sector (domain of activity), assigned with org:classification into the sop:OrganisationSector SKOS scheme [[SKOS]], whose top concepts include sop:Government (federal / state / local; agency, statutory authority, court, regulator), sop:Education, sop:Health, sop:NonProfit (charity, NGO, community legal centre), sop:Commercial, sop:SocialEnterprise, sop:Cooperative, sop:Community (clubs and associations), and sop:International (multilateral and DPI bodies). An organisation MAY hold several; institutions MAY extend the scheme.
  • Legal form, via rov:orgType (e.g. company limited by guarantee, incorporated association, statutory body), aligned with ISO 20275 Entity Legal Form codes.
  • Control / funding, via sop:controlType: one of sop:Public, sop:Private, or sop:Mixed.

Service delivery and contracting chains

When one organisation delivers a service on behalf of another, the relationship is made explicit and specialises PROV delegation (prov:actedOnBehalfOf):

sop:ServiceMandate (⊑ prov:Delegation)
A reified arrangement under which a sop:deliveringParty delivers a service on behalf of a sop:principalParty. It carries a sop:deliveryInstrument, a sop:mandateScope (the functions or services covered), a validity time:Interval, and — critically — an sop:accountableParty: the organisation that remains answerable to the person for the service, which outsourcing does not transfer by default.
sop:deliveryInstrument → sop:DeliveryInstrument
One of sop:Contract, sop:Subcontract, sop:Grant, sop:StatutoryDelegation, sop:MemorandumOfUnderstanding, sop:Licence, or sop:Franchise.
sop:actsOnBehalfOf (⊑ prov:actedOnBehalfOf)
A direct link from a delivering organisation (or one of its actors) to its principal. Mandates compose: a subcontracting chain (principal → prime → subcontractor) is a chain of sop:ServiceMandates traceable to the tier that actually acted.
<https://agency.example> a sop:Organisation ;
    org:classification sop:Government ; sop:controlType sop:Public ;
    sop:orgIdentifier "lei:5493…" .

<https://provider.example> a sop:Organisation ;
    org:classification sop:Commercial ; sop:controlType sop:Private .

<#mandate-1> a sop:ServiceMandate ;
    sop:principalParty     <https://agency.example> ;
    sop:deliveringParty    <https://provider.example> ;
    sop:deliveryInstrument sop:Contract ;
    sop:mandateScope       "employment services delivery" ;
    sop:accountableParty   <https://agency.example> ;   # principal remains accountable
    prov:startedAtTime     "2025-07-01T00:00:00+10:00"^^xsd:dateTime .

Jurisdiction, Oversight & Redress

Authority is territorial and layered, and the point of holding a verifiable record is often to take it somewhere for review. This section gives records and parties a jurisdiction, types the bodies whose function is oversight or enforcement, and links a determination to the pathway by which it can be challenged — turning an evidence container into an access-to-justice instrument.

Jurisdiction: states, territories, levels

sop:Jurisdiction is a skos:Concept in the sop:JurisdictionScheme, keyed to ISO 3166 codes: the Commonwealth of Australia (AU), its states and territories via ISO 3166-2 (AU-NSW, AU-VIC, AU-QLD, AU-SA, AU-WA, AU-TAS, AU-ACT, AU-NT), down to local government areas and up to supranational and international bodies. Each carries a sop:jurisdictionLevel: sop:Federal, sop:State, sop:Territory, sop:Local, or sop:Supranational.

A record or party links to its jurisdiction with sop:hasJurisdiction (the authority competent over it) and, where relevant, sop:governingJurisdiction (the law under which it is held and governed). This aligns with schema:jurisdiction and the Core Public Service Vocabulary [[CPSV]].

Oversight & enforcement bodies

sop:OversightBody is a sop:Organisation whose function is oversight, review, or enforcement. Its function is typed by the sop:OversightFunction scheme:

  • sop:Ombudsman — parliamentary and industry ombudsmen (a Commonwealth or state ombudsman; a telecommunications or energy-and-water ombudsman).
  • sop:LawEnforcement — police and enforcement agencies, federal and state.
  • sop:Court, sop:Tribunal — judicial and merits-review bodies.
  • sop:Regulator — sectoral regulators.
  • sop:IntegrityCommission — anti-corruption and integrity bodies.
  • sop:HumanRightsCommission — human-rights, anti-discrimination, and equal-opportunity bodies.
  • sop:InformationCommissioner — privacy, information, and freedom-of-information oversight.
  • sop:AuditorGeneral, sop:InspectorGeneral — audit and inspection.

Oversight is recursive: an enforcement body is itself overseen (police by an integrity commission or ombudsman). sop:overseenBy links an organisation or service to the body that oversees it, so the chain of oversight is itself in the record.

Redress pathways

A determination is linked to how it can be challenged:

sop:reviewableBy
The body competent to review a determination or hear a complaint about it (a tribunal, court, or ombudsman), within a stated sop:hasJurisdiction.
sop:reviewTimeLimit
Any statutory time limit within which review or complaint must be sought (a time:Duration or a deadline), so the person is not silently timed out.
sop:redressPathway
An ordered pathway where review is staged: internal review → external merits review → ombudsman → court.

Legal Instruments & Obligations

The obligations Solid-Databox enforces are not invented here; they exist in law. This section binds each obligation to the instrument that creates it, so a “MUST provide” can be traced to a treaty article or a section of an Act, and a person can see the legal ground of an entitlement.

Human-rights instruments are sourced from the Web Civics namespace [[WEBCIVICS]] (prefix wc:), which already codifies the international corpus — the UDHR, the ICCPR and ICESCR, the CRC, CAT, CEDAW, the CRPD, the ILO conventions and others — and whose core Jural, Jurisdiction, and Agency / Agent Accountability vocabularies this specification aligns with and defers to. Consistent with that namespace, human-dignity classifications attach to the natural person and are disjoint from institutional constructs. The and party models above should be read as mapping onto the WebCivics jural and jurisdiction terms, not replacing them.

Identifying an instrument

A sop:LegalInstrument (a treaty, Act, regulation, or charter) and its constituent sop:Provision (an article, section, or Australian Privacy Principle) are identified with the European Legislation Identifier [[ELI]] (prefix eli:), which is FRBR-based and dereferenceable, alongside the jurisdiction's own register (for the Commonwealth, the Federal Register of Legislation). An instrument carries its sop:hasJurisdiction and its enabling authority.

Binding obligations to what must be provided

Obligations are modelled as jural relations (aligning with the WebCivics Jural vocabulary): a sop:Right held by a person correlates with a sop:Duty borne by a duty-bearer. Two properties connect law to mechanism:

sop:mandatedBy
Links a record type, access right, or protocol obligation to the sop:Provision that requires it — e.g. the retrieval right is sop:mandatedBy the access principle of the applicable privacy law.
sop:dischargedBy
The inverse view: the Solid-Databox mechanism by which a duty-bearer discharges a statutory duty, producing an auditable record that the obligation was met — or, by its absence, was not.

Instruments to be codified

The WebCivics corpus covers the international layer; the following domestic instruments are not yet present and are to be added, each as a sop:LegalInstrument with its provisions, following the WebCivics pattern (/institutions/[authority]/[instrument-slug]/ served as Turtle and JSON-LD):

  • Privacy & records: the Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) and the Australian Privacy Principles — especially APP 12 (access to personal information) and APP 13 (correction), which directly ground the retrieval and correction protocol; the My Health Records Act 2012, the Freedom of Information Act 1982, and state equivalents (e.g. the Health Records Act 2001 (Vic), the PPIP Act 1998 (NSW)).
  • Australian human-rights instruments: the Australian Human Rights Commission Act 1986; the Racial, Sex, Disability, and Age Discrimination Acts; and the domestic human-rights statutes — the Charter of Human Rights and Responsibilities Act 2006 (Vic), the Human Rights Act 2004 (ACT), and the Human Rights Act 2019 (Qld).
  • Domain Acts: the social-security, child-protection, or telecommunications legislation a given deployment relies upon, added the same way and cited by the domain profiles.
<https://w3id.org/solid-databox/right/personal-records-access>
    a sop:Right ;
    sop:mandatedBy <https://www.legislation.gov.au/C1988A00119#app12> .   # Privacy Act 1988, APP 12 (access)

<#retrieval> a sop:Duty ;
    sop:dischargedBy <#solid-databox-retrieval> ;
    sop:hasJurisdiction <https://ns.webcivics.net/jurisdiction/AU> .

Conduct, Coercion & Risk

People inside institutions sometimes do wrong — and sometimes they do wrong because they were made to, by a superior or by an external party. Treating every act as freely willed erases the coerced worker as surely as anonymity erases the accountable one. This section models attestations about conduct, so a free wrong and a coerced one can be told apart, liability can be located, and the line between insurable and uninsurable risk can be drawn. It aligns with the WebCivics Agent Accountability and Jural vocabularies [[WEBCIVICS]].

sop:ConductAttestation (a Verifiable Credential [[VC-DATA-MODEL-2.0]])
A signed claim, by an identified or pseudonymous actor, that an act occurred, was observed, or was instructed — the evidentiary unit of this section.
sop:intent → sop:IntentLevel
The asserted state of mind behind an act: sop:Intentional, sop:Reckless, sop:Negligent, or sop:Innocent — what most sharply separates insurable from uninsurable risk.
sop:instructedBy, sop:coercedBy
The act was performed on the instruction of, or under coercion by, another actor — internal (a superior in the same organisation) or external. These specialise prov:actedOnBehalfOf and link into the delivery and participation chains, so pressure can be traced to its source.
sop:underDuress (xsd:boolean)
That the actor asserts they acted under duress — a claim which, if made out, is a defence and shifts responsibility toward the coercer.
sop:riskClass → sop:RiskClassification
One of sop:Insurable (accident, negligence), sop:Uninsurable (intentional wrongdoing, which public policy will not indemnify), or sop:LiabilityShifted (the actor was coerced, and responsibility moves to the party that compelled the act).

These are assertions, not adjudications: the vocabulary lets parties state and prove them; whether duress is made out, or an act was intentional, is for a tribunal or court (see ). The zero-knowledge machinery in the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]] governs how such claims are proved without over-disclosure.

The Category Scheme (Extensibility)

The function categories above form a SKOS concept scheme [[SKOS]], sop:RecordCategoryScheme, whose members are skos:Concept instances. An institution MAY publish its own scheme that skos:broadMatches these categories — adding, say, a “child-protection order” or a “telecommunications complaint” category — so local record types remain interoperable without being hard-coded here.
