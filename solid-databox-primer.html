Solid-Databox is a Habeas Data Egress building block: institution-run infrastructure that delivers verifiable copies of the records an institution holds about a person to that person, over open standards, with an audit trail. This is the non-normative companion to the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]]. It explains the problem Solid-Databox addresses, why the operational burden is placed deliberately on institutions, the architecture at a glance, and the project’s position within Digital Public Infrastructure. It states no conformance requirements; those live in the protocol.

This is an unofficial draft with no standing at the W3C, the Solid Community Group, or any other body. It is published to structure work toward the Solid 2026 Hackathon and to invite review. The project name is Solid-Databox; “Habeas Data Egress” names its function as a Digital Public Infrastructure building block.

The Problem

Institutions — government agencies, education and health providers, and others — routinely create and hold records that determine a person’s access to services, legal standing, employment, and health. Individuals are just as routinely denied a timely, complete, verifiable copy of those records. The underlying harm is not merely inconvenience: it is false claims acted upon. When a record is wrong, incomplete, or misleading and the person it concerns holds no copy, they cannot verify it, cannot contest it, and cannot use it in their own defence. The consequences reach from wrongful administrative debts to family-court determinations made on incomplete information.

The Realignment

Earlier decentralised-web efforts asked natural persons to run their own infrastructure — to become their own systems administrators in order to defend their own rights. That was a structural miscalculation. The parties that hold the data, the budgets, and the legal obligations are the institutions, not the individuals harmed by them.

Solid-Databox therefore places the engineering burden where the resources already sit. Institutions run the egress infrastructure inside their own environments; the individual’s side of the exchange is intentionally thin — authenticate, retrieve, and, where a record is false, contest. Once that plumbing exists, a consumer market for personal knowledge software can form on the far side of the pipe. This is a realignment of method, not of purpose.

Roles & Architecture at a Glance

The protocol defines five roles and two directions of flow:

  • Issuer — the institution, depositing verifiable records.
  • Egress point — the Solid “post-box” that holds deposits for collection.
  • Issuer bridge — middleware connecting the institution’s existing systems of record to the egress point.
  • Consumer Agent — the person’s software (e.g. Webizen) that retrieves and imports records.
  • Holder — the natural person the records concern.

The outbound path runs Issuer → Holder; a governed return path runs Holder → Issuer for corrections and appends. Normative detail is in the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]].

What Institutions Run

The reference engine is the Community Solid Server (CSS), deployable in two profiles from the same artefacts: a secure-by-default docker-compose bundle for non-profits and small services with little IT capacity, and Helm charts for agencies running Kubernetes behind API gateways. Both expose the same protocol surface.

A middleware bridge connects existing systems without rip-and-replace: it maps Microsoft Entra ID (formerly Active Directory) authentication to Solid-OIDC [[SOLID-OIDC]] and WebID, transforms SQL rows into Verifiable Credentials on the way out, and — on the way back — stages citizen corrections on a staff-review queue rather than writing destructively to the system of record. At government scale the same bridge polls REST endpoints through the agency’s API gateway and routes corrections onto a durable event bus. The institution keeps its database and its directory; Solid-Databox is additive.

What Individuals Run

On the far side of the pipe, a personal agent (Webizen) authenticates, retrieves records addressed to the Holder, and imports them into a personal knowledge system (QualiaDB) under the individual’s own consent and privacy controls. This is where the “inforg” — the curated body of information about a person — is assembled and held. Solid provides interoperable transport; the personal vault does the heavy lifting of agency, provenance, and curation. Solid-Databox specifies only the egress interface, so any Solid-compatible consumer software can collect from it.

Access, Guardianship & Accountability

Not all records carry the same sensitivity, and not all people can act for themselves. Solid-Databox ties the grade of access a person is granted to the assurance of how their identity was established and authenticated. An account opened by traditional self-service — an email address and a password — is valid but self-asserted, and reaches only the lowest grade: enough to read low-stakes correspondence or fix a contact detail. Reaching health, legal, or child-protection records requires step-up to a verified digital identity, such as Australia’s myID (formerly myGovID) under the Australian Government Digital ID System, or an equivalent accredited provider elsewhere.

Guardianship is treated as a first-class relationship, not an afterthought: a guardian is an agent authorised to act for a protected person (the entity), under a recorded authority — parental status, a court order, an enduring guardianship — with a bounded scope, a full audit of who acted on whose behalf, and a clean transition when a child reaches majority or capacity is regained. The normative rules are in the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]].

Accountability runs both ways. An institution's own agents cannot be anonymous: for any call or interaction that concerns a person, every operator, supervisor, and automated system involved — including across transfers — is logged by a stable identifier and role, even where their name was never given to the caller. That identifier may stay pseudonymous, but it must be resolvable by the institution and, on lawful request, by a court. An automated or AI agent must be disclosed as such, never passed off as a person. The same holds for organisations: when a government service is delivered by a contractor or an NGO, the record names both the delivering party and the principal it acts for, and which of them remains accountable to you — outsourcing does not dissolve responsibility.

Authority is territorial, so each record carries its governing jurisdiction — Commonwealth, a state, or a territory — and a determination carries the route to challenge it: the tribunal, ombudsman, or court competent to review it, and by when. The evidence container thereby points to its own remedy.

Grounded in Law, Honest About Coercion

The obligations here are not invented — they exist in law, and Solid-Databox ties each to its source: the access and correction rights that underpin the whole design sit on the privacy law's access principles (in Australia, APP 12 and APP 13) and on human-rights instruments, the latter drawn from the Web Civics namespace that already codifies the international corpus. An entitlement can therefore be defended by reference to the instrument that grants it, not merely to this project.

It is also honest about wrongdoing. Sometimes the person who commits a wrong was made to — ordered by a manager, pressured by a contractor above them. The design lets conduct be attested and proved with zero-knowledge techniques, so a consumer can prove they were wronged without exposing their whole file, and a coerced employee can prove they were instructed — shifting liability to the party that compelled them — while staying pseudonymous until a court requires otherwise. That distinction, between a free wrong and a coerced one, is also the line between uninsurable and insurable risk, and it is what lets both wronged customers and pressured workers actually litigate.

Habeas Data Egress as a DPI Building Block

Internationally, this pattern is being funded and specified as Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI). Solid-Databox is designed to map onto GovStack building blocks [[GOVSTACK]] — credential management, identity, and data exchange — to interoperate with issuance flows such as OpenID4VCI [[OID4VCI]] used by stacks like MOSIP, and to act as an edge-dominant data exchange in which the citizen is the authorised conduit for their own data. Framing the work as a Habeas Data Egress building block positions it for DPI grants, government tenders, and institutional partnerships rather than a single-jurisdiction prototype.

Commercialisation

The core standards (Solid, JSON-LD, Verifiable Credentials, Linked Data Notifications) remain open-source public goods. Sustainable revenue accrues at the integration and tooling layers — the shape of Red Hat around Linux, or managed cloud around open primitives:

  • Enterprise egress middleware — the Entra/AD bridge, SQL-to-VC transformation, and staff-review workflow, delivered as supported software.
  • High-assurance auditing — tamper-evident logs and telemetry that prove a data request was fulfilled lawfully and on time.
  • Citizen-side tooling — Webizen, QualiaDB, and local personal agents, offered as consumer products without exposing data to external clouds.

The Open Question: Evidence, Not Compliance Theatre

An egress point run by the institution can also choose not to deposit. So the audit guarantees only defend a right if either (a) deposit is triggered at the moment a record is created, rather than left discretionary, or (b) the Holder side can independently prove that an expected record is absent. Which of these Solid-Databox mandates is the decision that separates a rights instrument from tooling that merely documents institutions already willing to comply. It is tracked as a normative open question in the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]].

The design intent is that a delivered copy becomes a “point of no return”: once a Holder holds a verifiable record, later institutional alteration or deletion cannot invalidate the copy they already hold, and the fact of alteration or non-response is itself logged.

Reading the Specifications

The [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]] is the normative core: the resource model, the deposit → notify → retrieve → correct protocol, authentication and assurance, access grades, guardianship, and the evidence & audit requirements. The [[SOLID-DATABOX-VOCABULARY]] defines the content categories and metadata — letters, notes, economic records, system data, and multimedia such as IVR and call recordings — and how a transcript or other derivative records its accuracy and provenance. Two further modules are expected to split into their own documents as they mature:

  • Identity as an Enumerated Cryptographic State — the model that treats identity as an explicit set of cryptographic identifiers keyed to distinct agents and entities, rather than a single sovereign identifier.
  • Non-Response and Deletion Logging — the evidence-preservation guarantees that answer the open question above.
