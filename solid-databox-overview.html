Solid-Databox is a Habeas Data Egress building block: institution-run infrastructure that delivers verifiable copies of the records an institution holds about a person to that person, over open standards and with an audit trail, so that consumer software can build the individual's own knowledge vault. This document is the front door to the project. It outlines the problem, the approach, the documents and tools that make up the project, the guarantees it makes, and where the work currently stands. For depth, follow the links: this is a map, not the territory.

Unofficial draft, with no standing at the W3C, the Solid Community Group, or any other body, published to organise work toward the Solid 2026 Hackathon and to invite review. The project name is Solid-Databox; “Habeas Data Egress” names its function as a Digital Public Infrastructure building block. The citizen-side software is Webizen and QualiaDB.

What Solid-Databox is

Institutions create and hold records that decide a person's access to services, legal standing, employment, and health, yet the person is routinely denied a timely, complete, verifiable copy of those records. The harm is not merely inconvenience: it is false claims acted upon — from wrongful automated debts to family-court determinations made on incomplete information — by people who hold no copy with which to verify or contest.

Solid-Databox answers this by putting the engineering burden where the data, the budgets, and the legal obligations already sit: the institution. An institution runs an egress point — a controlled Solid “post-box” — into which it deposits verifiable records addressed to the people they concern. A person's own agent retrieves them, and where a record is false, contests it. Once that plumbing exists, a consumer market for personal knowledge software forms on the far side of the pipe. The full argument is in the project proposal and the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PRIMER]].

Architecture at a glance

The protocol defines five roles and two directions of flow. Outbound (Issuer → Holder): an institution deposits verifiable records, through an Issuer bridge that connects its existing SQL and directory systems, into an egress point (a conforming Solid server); a Consumer Agent (Webizen) retrieves them for the Holder and imports them into a personal vault (QualiaDB). A governed return path (Holder → Issuer) carries corrections and disputes to a staff-review queue — never a destructive write. Normative detail is in the [[SOLID-DATABOX-PROTOCOL]].

What the project guarantees

The value is not the plumbing (a Community Solid Server plus middleware) but a small set of invariants that every conforming deployment must uphold. These are the project's promise, and what a conformance check tests:

  1. Machine proposes, human disposes — automated systems may only propose (cml:Proposed); only a signed authoritative human attests. No auto-attestation.
  2. Assurance gates access — a record's access grade sets a minimum level of assurance; a self-asserted account never reaches high-stakes records.
  3. No destructive correction — a citizen's correction is staged for human review, never applied straight to the system of record.
  4. No anonymous institutional actors — every operator and software actor, across transfers, is logged by a resolvable identifier; pseudonymity is allowed, anonymity is not; automated actors self-disclose.
  5. Outsourcing does not launder accountability — records name the delivering party, the principal, and who remains accountable.
  6. Evidence is preserved — deposits, deletions, and non-responses are logged; a delivered copy is independent of the egress point; where a derivative (a transcript) and its source disagree, the original governs and stays retrievable.
  7. Redress travels with the record — a determination names the competent review body and the statutory time limit.
  8. Obligations cite their law — each record type is bound to the instrument and provision that require it.

Standards foundation

Solid-Databox reuses, rather than reinvents: the Solid Protocol [[SOLID-PROTOCOL]] and the Linked Data Platform [[LDP]] for storage and transport; Linked Data Notifications [[LDN]] for delivery; Verifiable Credentials [[VC-DATA-MODEL-2.0]] for the payloads; SOLID-OIDC [[SOLID-OIDC]] bridged to institutional identity (Microsoft Entra ID, myID) for authentication; ELI [[ELI]] for legislation identity and the WebCivics cml:/values: corpus [[WEBCIVICS]] for the human-rights and deontic layer; and the GovStack building-block model [[GOVSTACK]] for Digital Public Infrastructure alignment.

Status & roadmap

Honest state of the work:

  • Built — the proposal (v1.3.0); this overview, the primer, the protocol, and the vocabulary; the deployment-kit blueprint; and the legis2cml tool.
  • Verified — legis2cml's parsing and CML/values serialization are tested. The ReSpec documents have not yet been rendered/validated, and the tool's PDF and local-model paths are not yet exercised end-to-end.
  • Open decisions (see the protocol's issue boxes) — whether deposit is mandated at record-creation or absence must be provable; retention versus a right to erasure; who custodies the pseudonym-to-person mapping; and the per-jurisdiction access-grade tables.
  • Next — build the deployment kit (extract the vocabulary to a loadable sop.ttl, write the conformance checklist, then the agent files and per-institution profile), validate the specs, and codify the Australian instruments with legis2cml.
