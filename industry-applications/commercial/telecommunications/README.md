# Commercial: Telecommunications 

The telecommunications sector is a critical infrastructure layer that handles vast amounts of highly sensitive personal data, from identity documents to real-time location metadata. By integrating a Solid Databox, Telcos can shift from maintaining massive, vulnerable central databases (honey-pots) to a decentralized, trust-centric model that enhances consumer privacy and radically simplifies regulatory compliance.

## Core Telecommunications Data Payloads

In a Solid-enabled telco ecosystem, the databox serves as the secure exchange point for identity verification, billing, and highly sensitive metadata.

### 1. Identity & KYC (Know Your Customer) Onboarding
Telecommunications providers are legally obligated to verify a customer's identity before activating services to prevent fraud and criminal activity.
- **Instant Cryptographic KYC:** Instead of the customer uploading photos of their passport or driver's license—which the Telco must then securely store and protect—the customer grants the Telco temporary read-access to government-issued Verifiable Credentials (VCs) in their Databox.
- **Data Minimization:** Once the Telco verifies the cryptographic signature on the identity credential, they record the verification status in their system and immediately drop access. The Telco no longer holds the raw identity documents, significantly reducing their cyber-risk profile in the event of a breach.

### 2. Billing, Usage, & Proof of Address
The databox replaces the traditional customer portal, providing a secure, two-way pipeline for ongoing service management.
- **Verifiable Utility Bills (Proof of Address):** Monthly bills are deposited directly into the customer's Databox as Verifiable Credentials. These VCs serve as mathematically irrefutable "Proof of Address" documents, which the customer can instantly share with banks or government agencies, eliminating the need to download and email easily forged PDFs.
- **Usage & Metadata Control:** Call Data Records (CDRs), location metadata, and internet usage statistics are pushed to the Databox. This gives the consumer total visibility over their behavioral footprint and ensures the Telco cannot monetize this metadata to third parties without explicit, granular consent recorded on the Pod.

### 3. Service Management & Portability
- **Number Portability & eSIMs:** Cryptographic records of MSISDN (phone number) ownership and active eSIM profiles are stored in the Databox. When a customer decides to switch providers, they simply grant the new Telco read-access to their service credential. This completely automates and instantly executes the number porting process, eliminating legacy backend friction.
- **Anti-Smishing Support Channel:** Instead of sending unverified SMS messages for support tickets, billing reminders, or network outage alerts—which are highly vulnerable to phishing/smishing attacks—the Telco utilizes the Databox's secure messaging protocol. The customer knows with 100% certainty that the message is genuinely from their provider.

### 4. Subscriber-Level Number Blocking & Anti-Evasion
Harassment, stalking, and persistent unwanted contact frequently involve perpetrators acquiring new SIMs or alternative services to circumvent a simple block on a single MSISDN. A Solid Databox shifts the block from a per-number deny-list to a subscriber-scoped, evidence-backed directive that the Telco must honour through internal data-matching.
- **Subscriber-Scoped Block Request:** Rather than only blocking a specific phone number, the consumer deposits a signed "Block Directive" Verifiable Credential into their Databox that identifies the *subject* of the block (e.g., by name, prior MSISDN(s), and any corroborating evidence such as police report numbers or intervention order references). The consumer grants the Telco scoped read-access to this directive.
- **Internal Data-Matching Obligation:** Upon receiving the directive, the Telco performs internal data-matching against its own Customer Relationship Management (CRM), billing, and provisioning systems to identify any existing or newly provisioned service associated with the named subject—including services on a different MSISDN, a freshly activated pre-paid SIM, or a secondary account opened with variant identity details. The matching logic, confidence threshold, and audit trail are recorded against the directive so the consumer can later verify (via a signed receipt VC) that the obligation was discharged.
- **Pre-Emptive Blocking of Alternative Services:** When the Telco's data-matching identifies a match—or when a new service is subsequently provisioned that matches the subject—the Telco applies the block at the subscriber/network level rather than the call-handling level, preventing inbound contact from the alternative service before it ever rings the consumer's handset. This closes the well-known loophole whereby a blocked party simply buys a new SIM to resume contact.
- **Lifecycle Notifications:** The Telco pushes signed notification VCs to the consumer's Databox at each material event: (a) acknowledgement that the directive was received and data-matching commenced, (b) each identified match and the action taken (e.g., service suspended, calls barred), (c) escalation to law enforcement or regulatory bodies where the consumer has authorised disclosure, and (d) expiry or revocation of the directive. Because these notifications are Verifiable Credentials deposited into the Pod, they form an immutable, court-admissible record of the Telco's response.
- **Revocation & Scope Adjustment:** The consumer can amend or revoke the directive at any time from their Databox; the Telco is notified via the consent channel and must reconcile its internal block state accordingly, with a signed confirmation returned to the Pod.

### 5. Private Number Identification for Institutional Callers
A persistent consumer-protection gap is the use of "private number" / "caller-ID withheld" presentation by institutional organisations—banks, hospitals, government agencies, debt collectors, and emergency services. While legitimate, these calls are visually indistinguishable from fraud and scam calls, diminishing the consumer's ability to decide whether to answer. The Databox provides a verifiable side-channel that resolves this ambiguity without exposing the caller's raw number.
- **Institutional Caller Attestation:** An organisation that legitimately needs to withhold its CLI (Calling Line Identification) registers a "Verified Institutional Caller" Verifiable Credential, issued by a trusted authority (e.g., the regulator or a Telco-recognised attestation service). The VC binds the organisation's legal identity, the purpose categories it is authorised to call under (e.g., "fraud prevention", "clinical follow-up", "statutory notice"), and a rotating session identifier—*not* the underlying MSISDN.
- **Real-Time Call Announcement via the Databox:** When the organisation places a call to the consumer, the Telco signals the call over the secure Databox messaging channel *in parallel* with the inbound ringing event. The consumer's handset (or a companion Databox app) displays a verified card: the organisation's verified name, the purpose category, the session ID, and a cryptographic guarantee that the call originated from a service bound to the attested credential. The call still presents as "private number" on the traditional CLI, but the consumer now has authoritative context.
- **Consumer-Controlled Allow/Deny Rules:** The consumer can publish an "Inbound Call Policy" to their Databox declaring which institutional purpose categories they will accept (e.g., accept clinical follow-ups, decline debt-collection calls outside specified hours). The Telco consults this policy before completing the call path; non-conforming institutional calls are routed to a signed voicemail VC deposited in the Pod rather than ringing the handset.
- **Fraud Differentiation:** Because scam callers cannot produce a valid institutional attestation VC, any "private number" call that is *not* accompanied by a matching Databox announcement is immediately suspect. The consumer's device can surface a clear visual distinction: "Verified Institution: St. Vincent's Hospital — Clinical Follow-up" versus "Unverified Private Number — Do Not Share Personal Information". This restores the consumer's ability to discriminate between an important call and a fraud attempt without forcing legitimate institutions to expose their numbers.
- **Audit & Complaint Trail:** Every institutional call announcement is recorded as a signed VC in the consumer's Pod, creating an auditable history of who called, under what purpose, and when. If a call turns out to be a misuse of an attestation (e.g., a debt collector misusing a "statutory notice" category), the consumer can attach a complaint VC and escalate to the regulator with cryptographic evidence intact.

## The Solid Databox Workflow

1. **Secure Onboarding:** The customer initiates a new mobile plan. The Telco requests read-access to a verified identity credential (e.g., Digital Driver's License) in the customer's Solid Pod.
2. **Instant Activation:** The Telco verifies the credential's cryptographic signature, activates the eSIM, and immediately revokes its read-access to the raw identity data.
3. **Ongoing Egress:** Every month, the Telco deposits a mathematically signed invoice and usage summary directly into the customer's Databox.
4. **Seamless Porting:** When the customer leaves, they revoke the Telco's write-access and share their MSISDN credential with a competitor, taking their digital footprint and their phone number with them instantly.
5. **Subscriber-Level Blocking:** The consumer deposits a Block Directive VC and grants the Telco scoped read-access. The Telco performs internal data-matching, applies subscriber-level blocks to any matching or subsequently provisioned alternative services, and returns signed lifecycle notifications to the Pod for each action taken.
6. **Verified Private-Number Calls:** When an attested institutional caller contacts the consumer, the Telco pushes a parallel verified announcement to the Databox. The consumer sees the verified organisation and purpose alongside the "private number" presentation, and the call is screened against the consumer's published Inbound Call Policy.
