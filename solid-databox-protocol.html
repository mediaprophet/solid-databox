This document defines the Solid-Databox Protocol, a Habeas Data Egress building block: a standards-based interaction pattern by which an institution (an Issuer) deposits verifiable records about a natural person into a controlled, auditable egress point, and by which that person (a Holder) retrieves, and where necessary contests, those records. Solid-Databox builds on the Solid Protocol [[SOLID-PROTOCOL]], the Linked Data Platform [[LDP]], Linked Data Notifications [[LDN]], and the W3C Verifiable Credentials data model [[VC-DATA-MODEL-2.0]]. Its function is habeas data egress — the technical enactment of habeas data, the remedy (recognised in a number of jurisdictions) by which a person may compel access to and correction of records held about them.

This is an unofficial draft. It has no standing at the W3C, the Solid Community Group, or any other body. It is published to structure work toward the Solid 2026 Hackathon and to invite review. The project name is Solid-Databox; “Habeas Data Egress” names its function as a Digital Public Infrastructure building block. Normative statements are present but incomplete; sections marked with an issue box carry open design questions.

This specification defines three conformance classes: a conforming egress point, a conforming Issuer bridge, and a conforming Consumer Agent. A conforming implementation MUST satisfy every normative requirement stated for its class.

Introduction

Institutions routinely create and hold records that determine a person’s access to services, legal standing, employment, and health, yet individuals are routinely denied a timely, complete, verifiable copy of those records. This protocol addresses that asymmetry not by replacing institutional systems but by defining a uniform egress pathway out of them, so that a verifiable copy reaches the person the record concerns as a matter of standard practice.

The design deliberately places the operational burden on the institution, which holds the data, the budget, and the legal obligation. The person’s side of the exchange is intentionally thin: authenticate, retrieve, and — where a record is false or incomplete — contest.

Terminology

Issuer
An institution that creates records about natural persons and deposits verifiable copies into an egress point. Examples: a government agency, an education provider, a health service.
Holder
The natural person a record concerns, acting in a private capacity, who retrieves and curates records addressed to them.
Egress point
A Solid storage location [[SOLID-PROTOCOL]] configured as a controlled deposit-and-collection surface — the “post-box”.
Slot
A Linked Data Platform [[LDP]] container within an egress point designated for records addressed to a specific Holder.
Deposit
The act of an Issuer placing a verifiable record into a slot.
Consumer Agent
Software acting for the Holder (e.g. a personal agent such as Webizen) that authenticates to the egress point, retrieves records, and imports them into a personal knowledge system.
Agent / Entity
The agent is the party performing an action; the entity is the subject the action concerns. The two are kept distinct throughout (see ).

Architecture Overview

The protocol defines a one-way outbound path (Issuer → Holder) and a governed return path (Holder → Issuer). An Issuer bridge connects an institution’s existing systems of record to the egress point; a Consumer Agent connects the egress point to the Holder’s personal knowledge system. The egress point itself is a conforming Solid server.

Data Model & Vocabulary

The classes and properties that type deposited content — its modality, function, derivation, and metadata — are defined in the companion [[SOLID-DATABOX-VOCABULARY]], which reuses PROV-O, Dublin Core, schema.org, and the Verifiable Credentials data model [[VC-DATA-MODEL-2.0]], and coins terms only for the gaps. The provisional namespace is https://w3id.org/solid-databox/ns# (prefix sop:; TODO: register a w3id.org redirect).

Each deposit MUST carry provenance sufficient to identify the depositing Issuer, the time of deposit, and the addressed Holder, in a form the Holder can verify independently of the egress point.

A derivative — a transcript, translation, summary, or extracted dataset produced from an original record — is a claim about its source, not a replacement for it (see [[SOLID-DATABOX-VOCABULARY]]). A derivative MUST declare how it was produced (human, machine, or hybrid), the agent that produced it, and its verification status. An Issuer MUST NOT ground a determination on, and a Consumer Agent MUST NOT present as authoritative, a derivative at an evidentiary weight its verification status does not support: a machine transcript of a call is not the call, and where the two disagree the original governs and MUST remain retrievable.

Identity as an Enumerated Cryptographic State

Identity in this protocol is not a single identifier. It is an enumerated cryptographic state: an explicit, inspectable set of cryptographically supported identifiers (WebIDs, X.509 certificates, keys, and, where used, DIDs) together with their related datasets, organised on an agent-and-entity-centric basis that distinguishes the acting Agent from the subject Entity.

  • An implementation MUST NOT collapse the Agent and the Entity into a single identifier where they differ (e.g. guardian and child, caseworker and claimant, software agent and the person it acts for).
  • A deposit MUST bind to the Entity it concerns and record the Agent that performed it as distinct assertions.
  • The protocol SHOULD support a Holder presenting more than one identifier over the life of a record without loss of provenance continuity.

This is the technical expression of the human-rights position that human rights, not sovereignty, are the atomic unit of identity: an infant cannot be “self-sovereign” yet holds rights, and the agent/entity distinction is what preserves them.

Authentication & Assurance

Conforming parties authenticate using SOLID-OIDC [[SOLID-OIDC]]. The strength of an authentication is not binary; it is characterised by a Level of Assurance (LoA) with three independent dimensions, following established identity frameworks:

  • Identity proofing — how well the real-world identity behind an account was verified: weak for a self-asserted email address, strong for a biometric match to a government-issued photo credential.
  • Authenticator strength — how strongly each sign-in proves the same party has returned: a password, a phone, a hardware key.
  • Federation strength — how strongly a third-party assertion about the party can be relied upon.

These dimensions map onto national and international frameworks, so that Solid-Databox is portable across Digital Public Infrastructure: the Australian Government Digital ID System identity strengths — Basic, Standard, Strong [[?DIGITAL-ID-ACT]] — NIST’s Identity, Authenticator, and Federation Assurance Levels [[?NIST-800-63]], the eIDAS levels Low, Substantial, and High [[?eIDAS]], and ISO/IEC 29115. An implementation MUST record the LoA under which each identity was established and each request authenticated.

Verified digital identity

Where higher assurance is required, a party authenticates through an accredited digital identity provider. In Australia this is the Australian Government Digital ID System (AGDIS), reached through myID (formerly myGovID) and an Identity Exchange that brokers the assertion; comparable accredited providers exist in other jurisdictions. Such providers yield a high level of assurance — up to a biometrically verified strong identity — and so unlock the higher .

Traditional registration

A party MAY instead establish an account by traditional self-service means, for example an email address and a password, without third-party identity proofing. Such an account is valid but self-asserted: it carries the lowest level of assurance and MUST be confined to the lowest . Access to high-stakes records MUST require step-up to a verified digital identity; an egress point MUST NOT release a high-grade record to a self-asserted account.

Institutional identity providers (Entra ID)

Where an Issuer’s identity provider is an external OpenID Connect system, for example Microsoft Entra ID issuing OAuth 2.0 tokens [[RFC6749]], the Issuer bridge maps validated provider claims to a persistent WebID before acting against the egress point. Unattended machine-to-machine deposit MAY use the client-credentials grant, and MUST itself meet the assurance required of an Issuer.

Specify the normative claim-to-WebID mapping, the minimum validated claims for a deposit to be accepted, and the exact crosswalk from LoA to each national framework.

Access Grades

Records held about a person differ in sensitivity: a library membership and a child-protection file do not warrant the same protection. The protocol defines an ordered set of Access Grades, and each record class carries a minimum assurance requirement. Grade is a property of the record; the level of assurance is a property of the requester; access is permitted only where the latter meets or exceeds the former.

A conforming egress point:

  • MUST assign each slot or record class the minimum level of assurance required to read, append to, or contest it.
  • MUST deny a request whose presented assurance is below the record’s minimum, and SHOULD indicate the step-up needed rather than merely refusing.
  • MUST evaluate a Guardian’s request at the lesser of the Guardian’s own assurance and the scope their authority confers (see ).
  • MUST record, in the audit log (see ), the grade of the record and the assurance under which each access was granted or denied.

Illustratively: a self-asserted account may read low-grade correspondence and lodge a contact-detail correction; retrieving health or legal records requires a verified digital identity; and a guardian retrieving a child’s protection file requires both a strong identity and explicit authority of adequate scope.

Guardianship & Delegated Access

Not every Holder can act for themselves. Children, and adults under guardianship or with impaired capacity, hold rights over the records that concern them but exercise those rights through another party. The protocol treats this as first-class delegated access, not an exception — consistent with the position that human rights, not autonomy, are the atomic unit of identity (see ).

A Guardian is an Agent authorised to act in respect of a Protected Person (the Entity). A conforming implementation:

  • MUST represent guardianship as an assertion binding the Guardian’s identity to the Protected Person’s identity, distinct from either party’s own identity, and MUST NOT merge the two into a single account.
  • MUST record the authority on which the delegation rests — for example parental status, a court order, an enduring guardianship, or a power of attorney — as verifiable metadata.
  • MUST bound the delegation by scope (which records, and which actions: read, append, contest) and, where applicable, by time.
  • MUST log every action a Guardian performs as carried out by that Agent on behalf of the Protected Person, never as the Protected Person acting directly.
  • MUST support revocation and lawful transition — including a Protected Person assuming direct control on reaching majority or regaining capacity — without erasing the historical record of who acted, when, and under what authority.

A Guardian’s effective access is the lesser of their own level of assurance and the scope their authority confers (see ).

Accountability of Institutional Actors

Rights and accountability are symmetrical. Just as a Holder must be identifiable to exercise their rights (see ), an institutional actor must be identifiable to be answerable for what they do. Institutional agents — call-centre operators in particular — frequently give a caller only a first name or none, and a single interaction may pass through an IVR, several operators, a supervisor, and an interpreter. The protocol does not permit the institutional side of a record to be anonymous.

For any interaction (a call, IVR session, chat, or counter visit) that concerns a Holder:

  • The institution MUST record every participating institutional actor — human operator or software agent — by a stable identifier, together with the actor's role and the interval of their participation, using the participation model of the [[SOLID-DATABOX-VOCABULARY]].
  • Across transfers, each successive actor MUST be recorded and linked by a transfer event, so the full path of the interaction is reconstructable.
  • The identifier MAY be pseudonymous and need not be disclosed to the Holder, but MUST be resolvable by the institution — and, on lawful request, by an oversight body or court — to the natural person or software agent it denotes.
  • A software or AI actor MUST be identified as such, by name, version, and where applicable model; an egress point MUST NOT present, and an institution MUST NOT allow, an automated actor to be mistaken for a human.
  • An institution MUST NOT record a materially participating actor as “anonymous” or omit them.

This participation record is part of the tamper-evident audit trail (see ), and it is the institutional mirror of the agent/entity distinction: the operator is the acting Agent, the institution the Entity on whose behalf they act, and neither may be collapsed away.

Service Delivery & Contracting Chains

The organisation a person deals with is often not the one ultimately responsible: a government service may be delivered by a private contractor or a non-profit under a contract, grant, or statutory delegation, and that contractor may in turn subcontract. Outsourcing routinely launders accountability — the person is told the contractor merely follows the rules, and the principal that the contractor is responsible. This protocol forbids that gap at the record level.

Where a service or determination concerning a Holder is delivered under such an arrangement:

  • Each deposited record MUST identify both the delivering party and the principal on whose behalf it acts, and the instrument and scope of the arrangement, using the service-delivery model of the [[SOLID-DATABOX-VOCABULARY]].
  • The record MUST make explicit which party remains accountable to the Holder for the service; outsourcing does not transfer that accountability by default, and a person MUST NOT be left with no identified accountable party.
  • Where delivery is subcontracted across tiers, the chain MUST be traceable to the tier that actually acted, so responsibility can be located rather than diffused.

This is the organisational tier of the same principle as : a delivering body may not hide behind the principal, and a principal may not disclaim the acts of the body it engaged.

Jurisdiction & Redress Pathways

Authority is territorial, and a verifiable record is worth most when the person holding it knows where to take it. Australia's federation spreads competence across the Commonwealth, the states and territories, and local government, with a dense layer of ombudsmen, tribunals, courts, regulators, and integrity and human-rights commissions — and law-enforcement bodies that are themselves overseen. The protocol makes both the governing jurisdiction and the route to redress part of the record.

  • Every deposited record MUST declare its governing jurisdiction, using the jurisdiction model of the [[SOLID-DATABOX-VOCABULARY]].
  • A record that is a determination MUST identify the body competent to review it or hear a complaint about it, and any statutory time limit — so the person is told where and by when they may seek redress, and is not silently timed out. This encodes, as data, the review-rights notice that administrative law already expects such a decision to carry.
  • Access to a Holder's records by a law-enforcement or oversight body is itself a high-assurance, auditable event (see  and ): it is recorded, attributed to an identified actor and the authority relied upon, and — subject to lawful exceptions — visible to the Holder.

Because oversight is recursive, the body that reviews a determination and the body that oversees an enforcement agency are recorded on the same footing as any other party: no authority sits outside the record.

Legal Basis

Solid-Databox does not create rights; it operationalises those the law already confers. The obligations in this specification SHOULD be traceable to the instruments that create them, so an entitlement can be defended by reference to its source rather than to this document.

  • An egress point SHOULD expose, for each record type it makes available, the legal instrument and provision that mandate its provision (sop:mandatedBy, see [[SOLID-DATABOX-VOCABULARY]]).
  • A Holder's access or correction request MAY invoke a specific right — for records held in Australia, typically the access and correction principles of the applicable privacy law (APP 12 and APP 13) or a human-rights instrument — and the request and its disposition are logged against that ground.
  • Human-rights instruments are referenced from the Web Civics namespace [[WEBCIVICS]]; legislation is identified with the European Legislation Identifier [[ELI]] and the relevant jurisdiction's register.

Attestations, Zero-Knowledge Proofs & Risk

Some records are claims about conduct: that a wrong occurred, or that an actor was made to commit one. Two parties need to make such claims while disclosing as little else as possible — a consumer proving they were wronged without exposing their whole file, and an employee proving they were instructed or coerced without exposing themselves prematurely to the very party that pressured them. Conduct attestations (see [[SOLID-DATABOX-VOCABULARY]]) are Verifiable Credentials [[VC-DATA-MODEL-2.0]], and this section provides for their zero-knowledge presentation.

  • A party MAY present a conduct attestation as a zero-knowledge or selectively-disclosed proof — for example using BBS cryptosuites [[VC-DI-BBS]] — proving a predicate (that an instruction existed, that a role was held, that an amount exceeded a threshold, that a wrong of a stated type occurred) without revealing the underlying data or, where protected, the presenter's identity.
  • Where an actor asserts they acted under instruction or duress (sop:coercedBy, sop:underDuress), the proof MUST identify the compelling party to the extent needed to shift liability, while the coerced actor's own identity MAY remain pseudonymous, resolvable only by an oversight body or court (as in ). This is the whistleblower and coerced-worker protection path.
  • An attestation's risk classification (sop:riskClass) distinguishes insurable risk (accident, negligence) from uninsurable risk (intentional wrongdoing), and from liability that has shifted to a coercer — supporting litigation and indemnity decisions by either a wronged consumer or a compelled employee.
A zero-knowledge proof establishes that the presenter holds an attestation asserting a fact, and can prove predicates over it without full disclosure. It does not make the asserted fact true: its weight still rests on who signed the attestation and on corroboration (for a call, the original recording; see ). Zero-knowledge controls disclosure, not truth — the protocol must not let a cryptographically valid proof of a false attestation pass as proof of the underlying fact.

Protocol

Deposit (Issuer → Egress Point)

An Issuer deposits a record by performing an authenticated HTTP POST (or PUT) of a verifiable payload into the addressed slot, per [[LDP]]. The egress point MUST reject a deposit whose provenance cannot be verified, and MUST record every accepted deposit in the audit log (see ).

Notification

On accepting a deposit, the egress point MUST deliver a Linked Data Notification [[LDN]] to the Holder’s inbox announcing the new record. Notification MUST NOT include the record contents beyond what is required to locate and identify it.

Retrieval (Consumer Agent → Egress Point)

A Consumer Agent authenticates (see ) and retrieves records addressed to its Holder by standard Solid read operations [[SOLID-PROTOCOL]]. The egress point MUST NOT expose a slot to any party other than its addressed Holder and authorised Issuers. Retrieval is further constrained by : the Consumer Agent MUST present a level of assurance that meets each requested record’s minimum, and a Guardian acting for the Holder is bound additionally by the scope of their authority.

Correction (Holder → Issuer)

A Holder contests or appends to a record by sending a notification [[LDN]], or by opening a message thread bound to the specific record. A conforming Issuer bridge MUST NOT apply such a request as a destructive update to a system of record; it MUST stage the request for human review and record both the request and its disposition in the audit log.

Evidence & Audit: Non-Response and Deletion Logging

This section is what distinguishes a rights instrument from mere compliance tooling. An egress point run by the institution can also choose not to deposit. The requirements below only bite if either (a) deposit is triggered at the moment of record creation rather than left discretionary, or (b) the Holder side can independently prove an expected record is absent. Resolving which of these the protocol mandates is the central open question.

A conforming egress point:

  • MUST record every deposit, retrieval, and correction request in a tamper-evident log.
  • MUST record deletion of, or modification to, any deposited record, retaining evidence that the record previously existed.
  • SHOULD record the non-arrival of a record that a policy or schedule leads a Holder to expect, so that institutional non-response is itself observable.

A Holder’s retained copy, once delivered, is independent of the egress point: subsequent institutional alteration or erasure MUST NOT invalidate the copy the Holder already holds. This is the “point of no return” property — the protocol-level heir to the registered-correspondence model, where delivery could not credibly be denied.

Security & Privacy Considerations

Address: slot access control and enumeration resistance; minimisation in notifications; key rotation within an enumerated cryptographic state; handling of records concerning persons under guardianship; and the abuse case of a coerced Holder.

Relationship to Digital Public Infrastructure

As a Digital Public Infrastructure building block, Solid-Databox is positioned under the descriptor Habeas Data Egress. The egress point maps onto DPI building blocks [[GOVSTACK]] — credential management, identity, and data exchange — and interoperates with credential issuance flows such as OpenID4VCI [[OID4VCI]]. This relationship is non-normative and documented fully in the companion primer [[SOLID-DATABOX-PRIMER]].
